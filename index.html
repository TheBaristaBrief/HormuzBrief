What is a VLCC?

A Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) is one of the world's largest oil tankers, typically carrying 2 million barrels of crude oil per voyage. VLCCs are the backbone of Middle East oil export logistics.

The Day Rate is what it costs to charter a VLCC for one day. It is the most immediate real-time market signal of shipping stress — it moves faster than oil prices themselves.

Macro Impact — Scenarios Explained
GDP Impact
IMF · Allianz

−0.5 to −1.5 percentage points of global GDP growth lost per 3 months of sustained Hormuz disruption. This is based on IMF modelling and historical precedent (1973 oil embargo, 1990 Gulf War).

Why it compounds: Advanced economies face stagflation — rising energy prices with stagnating growth. Emerging market central banks are forced into rate holds or hikes even as growth slows, worsening the impact.

Former White House energy adviser Bob McNally: "A prolonged closure of the Strait of Hormuz is a guaranteed global recession."

Fiscal response limited: Allianz notes many governments lack fiscal space for meaningful intervention — oil tax cuts are the main tool. Allianz · IBTimes

Inflation Impact
Fed · ECB · Allianz

US (Fed study): Each 10% oil price rise adds +40bps to US headline inflation cumulatively over 8 quarters. With Brent already +54% vs Jan 5, the cumulative inflation impact could be ~216bps if prices are sustained.

Europe (ECB model): Each 10% oil rise adds +0.6% to EU inflation over 3 years. European gas at +104% adds a further shock.

Allianz: The conflict could delay Fed rate cuts to June 2026 (vs baseline March) and keep rates at elevated 3.75%. The DM/EM divergence is key: DM central banks look through initial spike; EM banks face sharper pressure as weaker currencies worsen import bills.

Food inflation lag (3–6 months): Higher energy and freight → higher fertiliser costs → higher agricultural input costs → food prices. Second-order impact most severe in SE Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa. Allianz

Non-Energy Sector Implications
Container Shipping & Logistics
Maersk · Linerlytica

Immediate: Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM, Hapag-Lloyd, COSCO all suspended. Greece advised entire merchant fleet to avoid Arabian Gulf. Emergency freight surcharge effective March 2 all GCC ports. ~170 containerships (450K TEU, 1.4% global fleet) trapped inside Strait. Cape of Good Hope rerouting adds 7–14 days + significant fuel cost.

If prolonged: Persistent schedule unreliability becomes the "new normal" — higher inventory buffers (working capital up), dual-sourcing, nearshoring, changes in ocean contract structures. Gulf transshipment hubs (Jebel Ali, Khor Fakkan) losing connectivity. Port and transshipment reconfiguration as alternative nodes gain share.

Most at risk: Importers/manufacturers with lean JIT inventory; low-margin retailers/CPG; Gulf port operators.

Advantaged: Forwarders/3PLs with disruption management; tanker owners during rate spikes; non-Hormuz maritime route operators.

Aviation, Travel & Air Cargo
Oxford Econ · IATA · Flightradar24

Immediate: 5,000+ flights cancelled in first 2 days. Complete airspace closure: Iran, Israel, Iraq, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, Syria. Partial: UAE, Saudi Arabia. 8–9 countries grounded fleets. Cost shock: reroutes add flight time and fuel burn while oil prices surge, squeezing margins. War-risk aviation insurance repricing at extreme levels. Crew and aircraft displacement takes days to recover even after closures lift.

If prolonged: Permanent rerouting of Europe–Asia traffic away from Gulf hubs. Middle East = 14% of global air transit — knock-on effects for Europe–Asia Pacific routes. Air cargo belly capacity lost. Gulf hub carriers (Qatar Airways, Emirates, Etihad) face structural revenue collapse.

GCC exposure: Gulf hub carriers most at risk — hub revenue collapsing while costs surge. Airlines with less Middle East dependency (US domestic, North America focus) advantaged.

Tourism & Hospitality
Oxford Economics Tourism Brief

2026 regional forecast: Middle East inbound arrivals −11% to −27% y/y = 23–38 million fewer international visitors = $34–56 billion visitor spend loss.

Country breakdown (y/y decline): Israel −57%, Iran −49% vs Dec 2025 baseline. GCC: Kuwait and Saudi see greatest y/y declines. Qatar (32% land arrivals) and Bahrain (74% land arrivals) most insulated from full airspace impact.

Recovery timeline: Early resolution (1–3 weeks): sentiment recovery through Q2–Q3. Two-month conflict: deeper damage lasting through year-end. Safety perception lingers beyond the conflict. Business travel disruption may persist longer than leisure.

Relative insulation: Bahrain 74% land arrivals = most insulated. Qatar 32% land arrivals = partially insulated.

Aluminium & Metals
LME · Industry Data

Gulf = ~10% of world aluminium supply. Bahrain's Alba (world's largest single smelter, 1.6M t/yr), Qatar aluminium, UAE smelters all in force majeure.

Restart takes 6–12 months once aluminium smelter furnaces power down — entirely regardless of conflict duration. Even a 2-week conflict creates months of lost supply.

LME aluminium: $3,322/t — approaching 2022 highs. Downstream users across automotive, aerospace, packaging, and construction face immediate cost shock.

Advantaged: Non-Gulf smelters (Australia, Iceland, Canada, Norway) capturing market share and pricing upside. Most at risk: Downstream aluminium users with Gulf supply dependency.

Chemicals, Fertilisers & Agriculture
Industry Data · NBC

Fertiliser: ~33% of global nitrogen fertiliser trade transits Hormuz. Urea now $625/t (up from $484). Northern hemisphere planting season underway — fertiliser delays now risk 2027 harvest shortfall.

Petrochemicals: Higher oil + LNG raises costs across entire petrochemical chain. Margin compression immediate where pass-through is slow. European and Asian curtailments possible if prolonged. Shift of production to advantaged gas-cost regions (Middle East, US).

Food inflation lag (3–6 months): Sustained fertiliser cost rises transmit to agricultural input costs. Food security risk most acute in South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa. Shipping and insurance disruption adds friction to chemical cargo (hazmat compliance).

Manufacturing & Consumer Goods
NBC · Business Insider · Supply Chain

Immediate: Freight + lead time shock across all manufacturing. Route diversions increase landed costs. JIT supply chains disrupted — automotive, consumer electronics, pharmaceuticals, medical supplies, temperature-controlled goods all affected (NBC, Business Insider). Working capital requirements rising across the board.

Energy-driven cost inflation: Higher oil and power affects manufacturing OPEX and inbound materials (plastics, packaging, chemicals). Consumer demand slowdown as energy-driven inflation feeds through to retail prices.

If prolonged: Supply chain redesign — more nearshoring/dual sourcing, higher inventory norms, contract changes (indexation to freight/energy). Demand risk: persistent inflation softens discretionary consumption.

Most at risk: Low-margin retailers/CPG unable to pass through freight + energy costs quickly.

Insurance & Financial Services
Reuters · The Guardian · WFW · CFR

War-risk repricing immediate and steep: Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, London P&I Club, American Club cancelled Gulf coverage effective March 5. Reuters reports premium hikes up to ~50% in some cases. Where still available: $150K–360K per voyage. VLCC voyage now ~$5M vs $3M last week.

Claims risk across: Hull & machinery, cargo, liability, business interruption, political violence and terrorism covers all elevated. Coverage disputes increasing — force majeure, war exclusions, "held covered" clauses becoming contentious as incidents accumulate.

DFC limitations (CFR): Legal restrictions (environmental/social compliance required), finite budget, cannot feasibly insure all maritime trade. Application approval takes time. Even insured ships face owners unwilling to risk hull loss in a war zone.

Structural growth: Political risk, maritime intelligence, security advisory, KYC/KYV for shadow fleet screening, AIS anomaly detection — all expanding rapidly.

Defence & Critical Infrastructure
Market Signals · Industry

Defence equities outperforming as markets price higher procurement and replenishment. Immediate demand rising for: counter-UAS systems, maritime surveillance, GPS-resilient navigation, air defence. UAE air defences described as performing impressively against volume of drone and missile attacks.

If prolonged: Sustained procurement tailwinds — munitions, air defence, drones, C4ISR, maritime security, plus multi-year maintenance/software streams. Cyber escalation risk: sanctions pressure and kinetic conflict can expand cyber campaigns against energy, logistics, and finance.

GCC procurement signal: All GCC states expected to pivot permanently from restraint-based strategy toward deterrence posture and military procurement acceleration — long-term structural demand for defence systems regardless of conflict duration.

Most at risk: Non-priority defence primes if budgets rotate toward drones, air defence, and software over legacy platforms.
